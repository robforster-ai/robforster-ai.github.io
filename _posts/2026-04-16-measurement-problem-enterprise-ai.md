Most enterprise AI programmes cannot answer whether they are delivering value — not because the technology is failing, but because they were never designed to answer that question. There is a question that every enterprise AI programme will eventually have to face: after the initial enthusiasm, after the pilot has been declared a success, after the business case has been approved and the programme has entered its delivery phase — someone in the executive team will ask how they know this is working. The honest answer, in most programmes I have encountered, is that they do not.

Not because the team is not capable. Not because the technology is not functioning. But because success metrics were an afterthought rather than a foundation. The programme was designed to build a thing. It was not designed to prove the thing was worth building.

> The time to define what success looks like is before you build, not after. By the time you are in production, the politics of measurement have already hardened.

This is a more serious problem than it might initially appear. Enterprise AI programmes operate under significant scrutiny. They are expensive. They involve organisational change. They touch processes that people have relied on for years. When the inevitable challenges arise — and they always do — the programme needs to be able to demonstrate progress and hold its ground under pressure. A programme that cannot measure its own impact cannot defend itself, and a programme that cannot defend itself will not survive the first difficult conversation with a sceptical CFO or a disappointed operations director.

The most common mistake is confusing **activity metrics** with **outcome metrics**. Programmes routinely track the number of models deployed, the volume of transactions processed, system uptime, and user adoption rates. These are useful operational indicators, but they do not answer the question the business actually cares about: what has changed, and what is it worth? A programme that has deployed five models processing ten thousand transactions a day at ninety-two percent uptime may still have delivered no measurable business value if those models are not changing decisions or improving outcomes in ways that connect to financial or operational performance.

The second problem is **attribution**. Even when outcome metrics improve — processing time drops, error rates fall, customer satisfaction increases — programmes struggle to demonstrate that AI caused the improvement rather than process changes, staff training, seasonal variation, or other concurrent initiatives. Without a measurement framework designed from the outset, the programme ends up arguing for credit it cannot definitively claim. That argument, made retrospectively, rarely lands well.

The discipline I have found most effective is to define the measurement framework before scoping the solution — not as a reporting exercise, but as a design constraint. If you cannot articulate at the point of business case approval how you will know in twelve months whether the programme has worked, then either the business case is not robust enough or the scope needs to be tightened until it is. A use case that cannot be clearly measured should not be prioritised above one that can.

This requires a different conversation with business stakeholders than most AI teams are accustomed to having. It means agreeing on baseline data before the programme starts, which requires process owners to accept that the current state needs to be documented honestly — something that is more politically sensitive than it sounds. It means accepting that some improvements will be shared with other initiatives, and establishing in advance how attribution will be handled. It means committing to outcome targets that the business will hold the programme accountable to, which is uncomfortable, but which is the only basis on which long-term credibility is built.

The programmes that navigate this well share a characteristic that is easy to overlook from the outside: they invest more time at the front end understanding what success means than most organisations think is warranted. It looks like slow preparation. It is, in practice, the work that makes everything downstream faster, cleaner, and more defensible when the scrutiny comes. And it always comes.
